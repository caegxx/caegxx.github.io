A companion piece on Flight Duty Period (FDP) construction for regional carriers, published earlier, examined how single-aircraft regional rotations get constrained by sector count and report time. That analysis covers most of the operational territory for narrowbody operators flying within Southeast Asia or any comparable regional market. It does not, however, address the very different problem that long-haul and ultra-long-range (ULR) operators face: how to legally schedule a single duty period that may exceed 17, 19, or even 22 hours from report to engines-off, crossing multiple time zones and the Window of Circadian Low more than once.

The constraints are different, the regulatory mechanisms are different, and the operational design choices (rest facility class, number of pilots, layover policy) are different. This article walks through the long-haul and ULR FDP framework, drawing on FAR Part 117 and CCAR-121 R8 as the authoritative reference schemes, and uses Singapore Airlines' A350-900ULR operations and Qantas' Project Sunrise as worked examples of where the framework reaches its commercial outer limits.

## 1. What changes when "long-haul" becomes the constraint

For regional operations, the dominant FDP constraint is the interaction between report time and sector count. A six-sector day at 07:00 hits a different maximum than a two-sector day at the same time.

For long-haul and ULR operations, the dominant FDP constraint shifts to four different variables:

**Total flight time itself.** Under FAR Part 117 Section 117.11, the unaugmented flight time limit is 9 hours (for daytime reports) or 8 hours (for early-morning or late-night reports). A 13-hour single sector cannot be flown unaugmented under any circumstances. The question is no longer "can the FDP fit?" but "how many additional pilots are required to fly this sector at all?"

**The crew complement and rest facility class.** Adding pilots beyond the minimum is no longer an optional tool for occasional long days; it is the structural design choice for the entire operation. The carrier decides at the network-planning stage whether each long-haul route operates with three pilots or four, and the certificate's Operations Specifications reflect this.

**Acclimatisation across multiple time zones.** A regional pilot operating from home base in their home time zone is automatically acclimatised. A long-haul pilot crossing 6 to 12 time zones in a single duty may not be, and Part 117 explicitly reduces the maximum FDP by 30 minutes for non-acclimatised operations (Section 117.13(b)).

**Consecutive nighttime exposure.** Long-haul rotations frequently expose crew to multiple consecutive duties crossing the WOCL. Part 117 Section 117.27 limits this to three consecutive nighttime FDPs under standard conditions, extendable to five if specific rest opportunities are provided. This is a constraint that simply does not apply to most regional operations.

The result is that long-haul FDP construction is fundamentally a fleet-level design problem, not a per-rotation check. The carrier determines its crew complement architecture, its rest facility configuration, and its layover policy at the strategic level, and individual rotations are then constructed within those choices.

## 2. The augmented crew framework under FAR Part 117

The headline mechanism enabling long-haul operations is augmented crew with designated in-flight rest. Under Part 117 Section 117.17 and Table C, an acclimatised augmented flight crew may operate a flight duty period substantially longer than the unaugmented Table B limits, with the maximum determined jointly by:

- The number of pilots in the augmented flightcrew (3 or 4)
- The class of rest facility available on the aircraft (Class 1, 2, or 3)
- The scheduled time of start (acclimated local time)

The complete Table C, for an acclimatised crew:

| Scheduled start (acclimated) | Class 1, 3 pilots | Class 1, 4 pilots | Class 2, 3 pilots | Class 2, 4 pilots | Class 3, 3 pilots | Class 3, 4 pilots |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 00:00-05:59 | 15 | 17 | 14 | 15.5 | 13 | 13.5 |
| 06:00-06:59 | 16 | 18.5 | 15 | 16.5 | 14 | 14.5 |
| 07:00-12:59 | 17 | 19 | 16.5 | 18 | 15 | 15.5 |
| 13:00-16:59 | 16 | 18.5 | 15 | 16.5 | 14 | 14.5 |
| 17:00-23:59 | 15 | 17 | 14 | 15.5 | 13 | 13.5 |

*Source: 14 CFR Part 117, Table C.*

The 19-hour peak (Class 1 facility, 4 pilots, 07:00-12:59 start) is the highest scheduled FDP permitted under Part 117 for any operation. Practically every published ultra-long-range schedule sits at or near this number.

Several structural points follow from the table:

**Class 1 facilities are essential for the longest FDPs.** Defined under Part 117 Section 117.3 as a bunk allowing flat sleeping in a temperature-controlled area separated from both flight deck and cabin, with control over light and isolation from noise, Class 1 facilities exist almost exclusively on widebody aircraft. The Class 1 advantage over Class 2 (a curtain-separated cabin seat with flat or near-flat sleeping position) is roughly 1 to 1.5 hours of additional FDP capacity. Over Class 3 (a reclining seat with 40-degree minimum recline plus leg support), the gap is roughly 2.5 to 3.5 hours.

**The 3-pilot versus 4-pilot decision is significant.** Adding the fourth pilot extends FDP by another 1.5 to 2 hours over a 3-pilot crew with the same rest facility. For schedules near the operational edge, this can be the difference between needing a fuel stop and operating nonstop.

**Section 117.17 imposes additional structural requirements.** Beyond Table C, augmented operations must satisfy: 2 consecutive hours of in-flight rest available for the landing pilot in the second half of the FDP; 90 consecutive minutes for the monitoring pilot during landing; and a maximum of three flight segments per augmented FDP. The three-segment cap rules out using augmented crew for multi-leg regional duties; this provision is specifically structured for the long-haul case.

**Flight time itself has separate caps.** Section 117.11 limits flight time within any augmented FDP to 13 hours with 3 pilots and 17 hours with 4 pilots, regardless of total FDP length. A 19-hour FDP with 4 pilots can include up to 17 hours of actual flight time; the remaining 2 hours are pre-flight, ground time, and post-flight elements that count toward FDP but not toward Section 117.11 flight time.

## 3. The CCAR-121 R8 framework

For Chinese-registered carriers, CCAR-121 R8 Section 121.483 establishes the parallel framework. The structural logic is the same, with slightly different specific values:

- Three-pilot augmented crew: maximum flight time 13 hours within the FDP
- Four-pilot augmented crew: maximum flight time 17 hours within the FDP
- Maximum FDP for augmented operations: built up from the prescriptive table and bounded by the augmented crew configuration

The CCAR R8 framework, like Part 117, treats the augmented operation as a structurally different category from the unaugmented Table B operation. A Chinese carrier scheduling its longest long-haul rotations works within the same conceptual framework: crew complement and rest facility class determine the maximum FDP, and the carrier's Operations Manual binds the planner to specific values.

## 4. Rest facility class: the often-overlooked design choice

Practitioners new to long-haul FDP planning sometimes treat the rest facility class as a fixed property of the aircraft type. It is not. The class is determined by the specific installation on the specific airframe, and a single aircraft type can be configured for different rest facility classes by different operators.

Part 117 Section 117.3 provides the formal definitions:

- Class 1: a bunk or other flat sleeping surface, located separate from both flight deck and passenger cabin, in a temperature-controlled area with flightcrew control of light and isolation from noise and disturbance
- Class 2: a seat in the aircraft cabin allowing flat or near-flat sleeping position, separated from passengers by at least a curtain to provide darkness and some sound mitigation, reasonably free from passenger or flightcrew disturbance
- Class 3: a seat in the cabin or flight deck reclining at least 40 degrees with leg and foot support

The operational implication: a 777-300ER with crew bunks installed in the "crown area" above the passenger cabin is Class 1. The same airframe operated by a different carrier without those bunks (using cabin seats curtained off in the rear) is Class 2. The same long-haul route can have a different maximum legal FDP depending on which operator is flying it.

For new fleet decisions, this matters at the procurement stage. A carrier ordering an aircraft for ULR service must specify Class 1 bunk installations to unlock the longest FDPs in Table C. Aircraft delivered without dedicated crew rest can operate the same routes only with shorter FDPs or with additional pilots (or, more practically, cannot operate the longest sectors at all).

## 5. Acclimatisation: the multi-time-zone problem

Acclimatisation is a binary classification with operationally significant consequences. Under Part 117 Section 117.3, a flightcrew member is acclimated when they have been in the relevant "theater" (a geographical area where flight duty period departure and arrival points differ by no more than 60 degrees longitude) for at least 72 hours, or has been given at least 36 consecutive hours free from duty.

The consequences of operating non-acclimated under Section 117.13(b):

- The maximum FDP from Table B is reduced by 30 minutes
- The applicable FDP is based on local time at the theater where the crew was last acclimated, not the current departure location

For a single ULR sector from a home base, the outbound crew is acclimated (they have been at home base for at least 72 hours) and gets the full Table B or Table C entitlement. The issue arises on the return: after a short layover at the destination, the crew may not have reached the 72-hour or 36-hour threshold at the destination, and may not have crossed back into the home theater. Under those conditions, the return FDP is reduced.

This is why carriers operating ULR routes generally schedule extended layovers at the foreign endpoint (long enough to reach acclimatisation under Part 117 definition) rather than minimum-time turnarounds. The economics of an extra 48 hours of crew downtime at destination are accepted as the cost of preserving full FDP capacity for the return.

## 6. Consecutive nighttime operations and cumulative limits

Long-haul operations frequently expose crew to repeated WOCL crossings. Under Part 117 Section 117.27, a carrier may schedule up to five consecutive flight duty periods that infringe on the WOCL only if the carrier provides a rest opportunity of at least 2 hours in suitable accommodation during each of those consecutive nighttime FDPs, meeting specific conditions equivalent to Section 117.15 split-duty rest. Without that rest provision, the limit drops to three consecutive nighttime FDPs.

The cumulative limits under Section 117.23 apply at the same numerical thresholds as for regional operations:

- Flight time: 100 hours in any 672 consecutive hours, 1,000 hours in any 365 consecutive calendar day period
- FDP: 60 hours in any 168 consecutive hours, 190 hours in any 672 consecutive hours

But the practical implication is different for long-haul operators. A long-haul pilot operating a typical pattern of one ULR pair per month (perhaps 38 to 40 hours of flight time in two long sectors plus connecting flying) hits the monthly limits much more slowly than a regional pilot flying multiple sectors daily. The binding constraint at the rostering level is more often the consecutive nighttime rule, the acclimatisation cycle, and the required 30-hour rest period within any 168 consecutive hours.

## 7. Worked example: Singapore Airlines A350-900ULR

Singapore Airlines' A350-900ULR operations, in service since October 2018 on the Singapore-Newark route, illustrate the framework at its commercial outer limit.

The published facts: SQ22 SIN-EWR is scheduled at up to 18 hours 25 minutes block time. SQ24 SIN-JFK is scheduled at up to 18 hours 40 minutes, with some flight plans showing block times approaching 19 hours 15 minutes depending on winds. The aircraft is the A350-900ULR variant with a 161-seat two-class configuration (Business and Premium Economy, no First or standard Economy), reflecting the operator's commercial decision to optimise for premium passengers over total seat count on these routes.

The FDP structure required to operate these sectors:

- Block time approaching 19 hours requires an FDP envelope at or near the 19-hour Part 117 Table C maximum (or the equivalent under Singapore's own civil aviation regulations, which Singapore Airlines operates under)
- A 19-hour FDP is achievable only with 4 pilots and a Class 1 rest facility under Part 117 Table C, scheduled to start in the 07:00-12:59 window
- The actual SQ22 departure from Singapore is typically in the late evening Singapore time, which means the equivalent acclimated start time for the operating crew depends on the layover and acclimatisation status at Singapore base

In practice, Singapore Airlines schedules the SIN-EWR/JFK operation with 4-pilot augmented crews using dedicated crew rest compartments (Class 1 facility). The 161-seat configuration reflects partly the structural reality that some space on the aircraft is allocated to crew rest rather than passenger cabin.

The route economics depend on premium yield, tailwinds (the westbound routings over the Pacific take advantage of the jet stream), and the operator's ability to amortise the dedicated A350-900ULR fleet over the limited number of city pairs it can serve. The FDP framework is not the binding constraint on whether the route is commercially viable, but it is the binding constraint on whether the route is operationally feasible at all. Without 4-pilot augmented crew and Class 1 rest, the SIN-EWR sector cannot be flown nonstop.

## 8. Worked example: Qantas Project Sunrise

Qantas' Project Sunrise programme, planned to operate Sydney-London and Sydney-New York nonstop using the Airbus A350-1000ULR variant, has reached a major development milestone. On 2 June 2026, the first A350-1000ULR (MSN 707, test registration F-WULR), the first of 12 aircraft ordered by Qantas, completed its first flight from Toulouse. The flight lasted 3 hours 43 minutes and reached slightly above 41,000 feet, operated by a dedicated Airbus Flight Test crew. The flight marks the start of a two-month flight test campaign to certify the modifications, including a new galley air-cooling system designed for very long flights. Following flight test, MSN 707 will be retrofitted to Qantas's commercial specifications. Delivery of the first commercial aircraft (the second A350-1000ULR off the line) has been pushed to April 2027 due to Airbus supply chain delays, with commercial service launch following thereafter.

The route distances tell the operational story:

- Sydney to London Heathrow: approximately 9,200 nautical miles
- Sydney to New York JFK: approximately 8,650 nautical miles
- Expected block times: up to 22 hours

The technical foundation enabling these distances is the integration of a rear centre tank (RCT) into the aircraft structure, which Airbus reports increases the range of the airframe by approximately 1,000 nautical miles relative to the standard A350-1000. The A350-1000ULR is positioned by Airbus as the fourth passenger variant in the A350 Family, alongside the A350-900, A350-900ULR, and standard A350-1000, and Airbus now describes it as the world's longest-range commercial aircraft.

A 22-hour scheduled block time pushes beyond any FDP envelope available under prescriptive rules. Even Part 117 Table C at its maximum permits only 19 hours of scheduled FDP. The Qantas operational solution must therefore involve at least one of the following:

- Operation under a Fatigue Risk Management System (FRMS) approved by the regulator (CASA in Qantas's case), allowing deviation from the prescriptive 19-hour ceiling provided the carrier demonstrates equivalent safety through fatigue modelling and continuous monitoring
- Crew configurations beyond 4 pilots, with additional rest facilities supporting longer in-flight rest periods
- Specific aircraft modifications (the A350-1000ULR is configured with 238 seats across four classes: 6 First Class Suites, 52 Business Class, 40 Premium Economy, and 140 Economy with 33-inch pitch, plus a dedicated wellbeing zone for passengers)

Qantas's order book reflects the two-tier fleet strategy: 12 specially modified A350-1000ULRs (ordered 2022) for Project Sunrise routes, plus 12 standard A350-1000s (ordered 2023) for other long-haul routes that do not require the extreme range. The capital commitment is substantial, and the schedule depends on the FRMS approval, the aircraft delivery cadence, and the certification of the modified type.

For network planners studying ULR economics, Project Sunrise illustrates that the operational frontier is now defined by regulatory mechanism design (FRMS approval) as much as by aircraft capability. The A350-1000ULR can fly the route; whether the route can be operated commercially depends on whether the regulator and the carrier can together design and implement a fatigue management system that maintains safety equivalent to the prescriptive rules.

## 9. Three principles for long-haul FDP planning

For carriers operating in the long-haul or ULR space, three working principles distilled from the framework:

**Principle 1: Treat crew complement and rest facility as a network-level decision.** Unlike the regional case, where augmented crew is an occasional response to an outlier day, long-haul augmented crew is the structural baseline for the operation. The carrier's network design, fleet design, and crew rostering all depend on the choice. Making this decision well at the network-planning stage saves substantial cost relative to discovering the constraint mid-implementation.

**Principle 2: Schedule layovers around acclimatisation, not around minimum time.** A 24-hour layover may seem operationally efficient, but if it falls below the Part 117 72-hour acclimatisation threshold or the 36-hour duty-free rest threshold, the return FDP capacity is reduced. Whether the carrier accepts that reduction or schedules longer layovers is a calibration question, but the calibration must be made consciously.

**Principle 3: Understand the FRMS option before assuming a route is infeasible.** Prescriptive Part 117 or CCAR-121 R8 limits define what can be done within the standard framework. Routes that exceed those limits are not automatically impossible; they are candidates for FRMS-based scheduling, with the substantial regulatory commitment that implies. The Qantas Project Sunrise model is instructive: extreme routes require extreme regulatory and operational infrastructure, but the path exists.

## Closing

Long-haul and ULR FDP construction is, in practice, the most operationally demanding intersection of regulatory framework and commercial design in aviation. The framework provided by Part 117 and CCAR-121 R8 is sufficient for the longest scheduled flights currently operating (Singapore Airlines' 18-19 hour Newark and JFK services), but reaching beyond that envelope (Qantas' planned 22-hour Sydney-London) requires the FRMS pathway, with its own demanding evidentiary and operational requirements.

For practitioners working in long-haul scheduling, the framework should not be a constraint discovered late in the design process. It should be a Day One input that shapes fleet selection, network design, crew complement architecture, and rest facility specification, alongside aircraft range, market demand, and slot availability. The carrier that integrates FDP framework thinking into commercial route planning at the strategic level operates with a real efficiency advantage over carriers where these constraints are treated as downstream compliance checks.

The regional FDP article published earlier on this site addressed the high-frequency, multi-sector case where sector count drives the binding limit. This article has addressed the long-sector, multi-time-zone case where flight time, rest facility, and acclimatisation drive the limit. Both cases share the same underlying framework, the same regulatory references, and the same practitioner discipline: read the regulations, understand the structural choices, plan around the limits rather than discover them late.
