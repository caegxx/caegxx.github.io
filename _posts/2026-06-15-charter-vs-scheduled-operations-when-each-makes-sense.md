The previous post in this series examined wet-leasing and ACMI operations as the operational flexibility layer that sits above an airline's primary fleet financing structure. A related but distinct question that frequently confuses both practitioners and outside observers is the difference between charter and scheduled commercial operations. These are two fundamentally different categories of air service under international law, with different regulatory permission structures, different commercial models, and different operational characteristics. Conflating the two, or treating the difference as merely a marketing distinction, is one of the more consequential errors a planner can make.

This article walks through what charter and scheduled actually mean as legal and operational categories, how the Chicago Convention framework structures them, the operating cases where each makes sense, and the boundary conditions where one can convert into the other.

## 1. The Chicago Convention split

The legal foundation for the charter-scheduled distinction sits in two articles of the Chicago Convention of 1944, the founding treaty of international civil aviation.

Article 5, titled "Right of Non-Scheduled Flight," establishes the principle that non-scheduled international flights enjoy a baseline right to fly into or across the territory of other contracting states without prior permission, subject to the right of the overflown state to require landing. This is the closest thing to a freedom of the air that the Convention grants automatically to commercial operators. The article explicitly extends this right to non-scheduled flights carrying passengers, cargo, or mail for remuneration, although the territorial state retains the right to impose "such regulations, conditions or limitations as it may consider desirable."

Article 6, titled "Scheduled Air Services," takes the opposite default position: "No scheduled international air service may be operated over or into the territory of a contracting State, except with the special permission or other authorization of that State." Every scheduled international service requires explicit authorization from every state it touches, almost always in the form of a bilateral air services agreement between the home and host states (covered in an earlier post in this series), plus an operating permit issued by each host state's civil aviation authority.

The difference between these two regimes is enormous in practice. A non-scheduled flight from Singapore to Hanoi can, in principle, be arranged with relatively limited regulatory process: the operator notifies the Vietnamese authorities, obtains landing permission, and operates. A scheduled flight on the same route requires the underlying Singapore-Vietnam bilateral ASA to authorize the service, the carrier to be designated under that ASA, an FAOC application to be completed and approved by the Vietnamese authorities, slot allocations to be obtained, and the carrier to publish a timetable that complies with the bilateral framework.

The friction between these two regulatory paths shapes much of how charter operations are actually structured.

## 2. What "scheduled" really means

The Convention itself does not provide a definition of "scheduled," which has historically led to some ambiguity. ICAO has filled this gap through its statistical and operational glossaries. The current ICAO definition characterises scheduled services as flights:

- Operated for remuneration according to a published timetable, or
- So regular or frequent as to constitute a recognizably systematic series, and
- Open to direct booking by members of the public

The three elements together capture the essence of scheduled service: a published commitment to operate on specific dates and times, a pattern frequent enough to be recognizably a service rather than an ad-hoc arrangement, and direct public access for ticket purchase. A flight that meets all three is unambiguously scheduled. A flight that meets none of them is unambiguously charter.

The interesting cases are in between, and they have driven significant regulatory clarification over the decades. A weekly charter flight that operates every Saturday for an entire summer season, sold by a tour operator to its customers as if it were a scheduled service, looks operationally and commercially like a scheduled service even though it lacks a published timetable in the usual sense. Many regulators apply functional tests to such operations, looking at whether the carrier is holding out the service to the public in a manner consistent with scheduled operation. When the test points to scheduled, the carrier may be required to obtain scheduled operating permits even if it commercially preferred to operate under charter authorization.

## 3. The charter taxonomy

Charter operations come in several distinct varieties, each with different regulatory and commercial characteristics:

Ad-hoc charter is the purest form: a single flight, arranged for a specific customer, on a specific date, between specific airports. The customer pays for the entire aircraft (block-time charter), not individual seats. Examples include corporate executive flights, sports team transport, and military or government movements. Ad-hoc charter operations face the lightest scheduled-service classification risk because the flight pattern is genuinely irregular.

Series charter is more complex. A tour operator may contract a carrier to operate the same route weekly for an entire summer season, with the tour operator selling seats to its customers as part of holiday packages. This is structurally still charter (the tour operator is the carrier's contractual customer, not the individual passengers), but the operational pattern looks much more like scheduled service. Regulators in various jurisdictions have at different times applied "functional scheduled" tests to such series charters, particularly when consumer protection or competition considerations arise.

Inclusive tour charter (ITC) is a specific subcategory of series charter where the seats are sold only as part of a packaged tour including accommodation. Many European holiday markets are built around this structure, with carriers like Condor, TUI fly, and Jet2 operating substantial parts of their networks under ITC frameworks. Asian regional aviation has historically used ITC less extensively, but Hajj and Umrah charter operations function similarly: pilgrims book complete packages including travel, and the carrier operates a series of flights specifically configured for that customer base.

Wet-lease charter overlaps with the ACMI structures covered in the previous post. A carrier that wet-leases capacity to another carrier for a specific purpose (covering a route during owned-fleet delivery delays, supplementing Hajj capacity, providing crisis recovery lift) is operating that wet-leased capacity as charter from the lessor's perspective, even if the lessee carrier is using it to operate what is effectively scheduled service. The regulatory treatment of this hybrid case varies by jurisdiction.

VIP and head-of-state charter operates under specialised frameworks. National carriers and specialist business aviation operators provide aircraft for government delegations, diplomatic flights, and senior executive movements, often with elevated security and protocol requirements that are absent from standard commercial operations.

## 4. What "scheduled" looks like operationally

Scheduled operations are structurally different from charter in several ways that go beyond the legal classification:

Scheduled operators commit to fly regardless of load factor. A scheduled flight from Singapore to Bangkok at 0800 on a Tuesday operates whether the booking class loads come in at 25% or 95%. The carrier may adjust capacity (changing aircraft type, cancelling at very low loads under specific circumstances), but the default expectation is that the flight operates. This load-independence is the core commitment of scheduled service.

Charter operators, by contrast, can decline to operate a flight whose economics turn unfavourable. An ad-hoc charter that suffers a customer cancellation simply does not fly. A series charter where the tour operator fails to fill the aircraft may renegotiate or terminate the contract. The financial risk model is fundamentally different.

Scheduled operations require permanent network capacity. A carrier operating 50 weekly Singapore-Bangkok rotations needs sufficient aircraft, crew, and maintenance capacity to sustain that volume continuously, plus reserve capacity to handle disruptions. Charter operations can use ad-hoc capacity, including wet-leased aircraft, to match specific contracts.

Scheduled operations are visible in industry distribution. Scheduled flights appear in global distribution systems (GDS), are bookable through online travel agencies, are aggregated in price comparison engines, and contribute to the carrier's reputation and brand visibility. Charter operations are largely invisible to consumer search and aggregation systems, distributed instead through tour operators, charter brokers, and direct relationships.

Scheduled operations face slot, traffic right, and capacity constraints that charter operations may avoid. A scheduled flight from Kuala Lumpur to Bangkok requires a slot at both ends, traffic rights under the Malaysia-Thailand ASA, and may face capacity quotas if those are specified in the bilateral framework. A non-scheduled flight on the same route may be exempt from some of these constraints, although the operating state can impose its own conditions under Article 5.

## 5. When charter is the right structure

Several operating cases naturally fit the charter model better than scheduled service:

Ad-hoc demand. When the customer is a single party (a sports team, a corporate group, a government delegation, a film crew) and the requirement is for specific point-to-point movement on specific dates, charter is the natural structure. Setting up a scheduled service to meet a one-off requirement is operationally and regulatorily disproportionate.

Seasonal demand concentrated in a short window. Hajj and Umrah operations are the canonical example. Demand peaks for a few weeks around the pilgrimage, with charter capacity supplementing scheduled flag carrier services for the surge. The 2026 Saudi Arabia Hajj activated airlift capacity for over one million pilgrim seats during the season, with national carrier Saudia coordinating directly with Hajj and Umrah Ministry authorities and supplementary charter capacity covering the seasonal surge. Operating these as permanent scheduled flights would leave aircraft underutilized for most of the year.

Inclusive tour markets with packaged distribution. Where the commercial model bundles accommodation, transfers, activities, and travel into a single product sold by a tour operator, the charter structure (with the tour operator as the carrier's customer) typically fits the distribution model more cleanly than scheduled service distributed through standard channels.

Market entry where bilateral traffic rights are constrained. A carrier wanting to test a route between two countries with restrictive bilateral capacity provisions may find that charter operations (which face different constraints under Article 5) provide a path to market that scheduled service would not. This is genuinely useful for new route exploration, but it carries the risk of being reclassified as scheduled if the operating pattern is regular enough.

Regulatory bridging during scheduled permit processing. The scheduled FAOC application process can take 60-90 days even under favourable conditions (covered in an earlier post on FAOC applications). Carriers with traffic rights but pending FAOCs sometimes operate initial flights under non-scheduled permits while the scheduled approval works through, then transition to scheduled operation once permits are issued. This bridging use is well-established and typically uncontroversial provided the transition is genuinely managed.

## 6. When scheduled is the right structure

The cases where scheduled service is the right structure are equally clear:

Permanent route service to known O&D markets. Routes with stable, recurring demand that supports daily or near-daily operation are not well served by charter structures. The marketing investment, distribution presence, and customer loyalty mechanisms that make scheduled service work do not transfer easily to charter.

Hub-and-spoke network integration. Connecting passengers between flights, codeshare partnerships, alliance memberships, frequent flyer programmes, and interline ticketing all depend on the operational predictability of scheduled service. Charter operations cannot easily participate in these network mechanisms.

Markets requiring published timetables for business travel. Business travellers need to book flights weeks or months in advance, with confidence that the flight will operate. The "open to public direct booking" element of the scheduled definition matches this requirement directly. Charter operations cannot meaningfully serve business travel markets except in specialised cases.

Routes with sufficient demand to fill scheduled capacity. Once weekly demand on a route routinely fills three or more flights, the operational efficiency of scheduled service (continuous utilisation of aircraft and crew, simpler distribution, brand visibility) substantially outperforms charter alternatives.

Markets where regulatory framework expects scheduled service. Bilateral ASAs typically address scheduled service explicitly, with traffic rights, capacity provisions, and designation criteria all written for the scheduled context. Charter alternatives may exist but face less developed regulatory frameworks and more uncertainty in administrative practice.

## 7. The boundary cases

Several operating cases sit awkwardly between charter and scheduled, and the choice of structure has significant consequences:

A carrier launching a new route may begin under charter authorization (faster to set up, less regulatory commitment), with the intent to transition to scheduled service if the route performs. The risk is that the regulator may view the charter operation as functionally scheduled if the pattern is regular enough, leading to reclassification disputes. Carriers managing this transition typically maintain visible distinctions in scheduling pattern, distribution, and public communication to support the charter classification.

A tour operator series running the same weekly route for an entire season may face the same risk in reverse: the regulator may treat what is contractually a series charter as functionally scheduled, requiring scheduled-grade permits and traffic rights. Some jurisdictions accept this hybrid status more readily than others.

A carrier providing wet-lease capacity to another carrier may be operating those flights as charter from the lessor's perspective, while the lessee uses the capacity to operate scheduled service to the public. The regulatory treatment of the lessor's role varies, with some jurisdictions requiring the wet-lease lessor to hold scheduled-grade authorizations and others treating the operation as a charter sub-contract.

The 2026 SpiceJet damp lease arrangement covered in the previous post illustrates one common resolution: the lessee carrier (SpiceJet) maintains its own scheduled AOC and operating authorizations, while the lessor provides aircraft, crew, and maintenance under a contract structure that does not require the lessor to hold scheduled authorizations in SpiceJet's operating market. The damp lease structure (lessee provides insurance and operates under own AOC) reflects exactly this regulatory boundary.

## 8. ASEAN-specific considerations

Charter and scheduled operations across ASEAN states involve a few region-specific considerations worth noting:

ASEAN MAAS framework applies primarily to scheduled service. The Multilateral Agreement on Air Services and its MAFLPAS extensions (covered in an earlier post) grant multilateral traffic rights for scheduled operations among ASEAN states. Non-scheduled operations remain primarily under Article 5 of the Chicago Convention, with bilateral relationships layered on top. Carriers planning charter operations in ASEAN should expect to negotiate bilaterally with each host state, even though the same carrier operating scheduled service in the same markets might benefit from multilateral MAAS coverage.

Hajj and Umrah charter operations are operationally significant for Indonesian, Malaysian, Brunei, and Singapore-based carriers, given the large pilgrim populations. The charter authorization process for Hajj operations is well-established in these markets, with annual approval cycles aligned to the Islamic calendar and typically involving the Ministry of Religious Affairs (or equivalent) alongside the civil aviation authority.

Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia each have charter-friendly regulatory environments for inbound tourism, particularly for charter operations bringing leisure passengers from origin markets in China, Korea, and Japan. The regulatory paths and approval timelines vary, and carriers planning such operations should work through local partners or general sales agents with current procedural knowledge.

Indonesia's geography (over 17,000 islands) creates a substantial market for domestic ad-hoc charter operations, particularly for tourism, oil and gas industry transport, and government movements. Several Indonesian operators specialise in this market, alongside the Garuda Indonesia and Citilink scheduled operations covered in the Hub vs Point-to-Point post.

## 9. Three rules for the charter-scheduled decision

For carriers evaluating whether a specific operating need fits charter or scheduled structure, three working rules:

**Rule 1: Match the regulatory structure to the actual demand pattern.** A flight pattern that is genuinely irregular, demand-led, and one-off fits charter. A flight pattern that is regular, public-facing, and demand-independent fits scheduled. Forcing a mismatch (charter authorization for what is functionally scheduled service, or scheduled service for what is genuinely ad-hoc) creates regulatory risk and operational inefficiency.

**Rule 2: Plan for transitions rather than indefinite hybrid status.** Carriers that begin under charter with the intent to transition to scheduled should manage that transition deliberately, with a defined timeline and explicit regulatory engagement. Indefinite hybrid status (charter operations that gradually look more and more scheduled, without ever formally transitioning) attracts regulatory attention and creates uncertainty about the operation's legal classification.

**Rule 3: Understand the host state's classification practice.** ICAO definitions provide the framework, but each national civil aviation authority applies the framework with its own administrative practice. The same operation that one ASEAN regulator treats as charter may be reclassified as scheduled by another regulator with different interpretive traditions. Engaging with the host CAA early in the planning process, and aligning the operational structure to that CAA's expectations, reduces classification risk.

## Closing

Charter and scheduled operations are not interchangeable labels for the same underlying activity. They represent fundamentally different regulatory regimes, commercial models, and operational characteristics, with each suited to specific use cases. Carriers that understand the distinction structurally, and design their operations to fit the regulatory category they are actually using, operate with real advantages over carriers that treat the choice as a casual administrative matter.
