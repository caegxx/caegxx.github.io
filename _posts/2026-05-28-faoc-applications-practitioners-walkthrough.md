When a carrier launches an international route, two documents matter most. The first is the **bilateral air services agreement** between the home and host states, which I covered in an earlier post. The second is the **Foreign Air Operator Certificate (FAOC)**, the host state's authorisation for a specific foreign carrier to actually operate. Without both, the route cannot fly, no matter how favourable the commercial economics or how confident the press release.

FAOCs are, in practice, the workstream that quietly absorbs the most calendar time on a new international route project. A route that launches 12 months after kick-off typically had its bilateral ASA in place months earlier, with the rest of the time spent on FAOC processing, technical document submission, inspection scheduling, and final certificate issue. Yet the process is poorly documented in English-language industry literature outside of Western jurisdictions. This article walks through what FAOCs are, what the typical six-stage process looks like, what documents are universally required, how the process differs across ASEAN civil aviation authorities, and where applications most often stall.

## 1. What an FAOC actually is

The FAOC is the host state's safety-oversight certificate for a foreign carrier intending to operate scheduled or charter commercial services into, out of, or over that state's territory. The conceptual basis sits in three articles of the Chicago Convention of 1944:

- **Article 29** requires every aircraft engaged in international navigation to carry specific documents (certificate of registration, certificate of airworthiness, crew licences, journey log, radio licence).
- **Article 32** addresses the recognition of personnel licences issued by the State of Registry.
- **Article 33** establishes that certificates of airworthiness and personnel licences issued by the State of Registry shall be recognised as valid by other contracting States, **provided** they meet or exceed the standards established under the Convention.

The operative phrase is "provided they meet or exceed the standards." Article 33 does not require automatic acceptance; it requires acceptance only if the issuing state's oversight system is judged adequate. The mechanism by which a host state makes that judgment, for any given foreign carrier proposing to operate into its territory, is the FAOC process.

The substantive safety standards the host state checks against are those in **ICAO Annex 6, Part I** (Operation of Aircraft — International Commercial Air Transport — Aeroplanes), Annex 1 (Personnel Licensing), and Annex 8 (Airworthiness of Aircraft). A foreign carrier whose home Air Operator Certificate (AOC) was issued under standards equivalent to these Annexes will, in the general case, find FAOC issuance straightforward. A foreign carrier from a state with known safety oversight deficiencies (often reflected in ICAO USOAP audit results or the EU Air Safety List) will find the process slower and more demanding.

**FAOC is distinct from the traffic right.** The traffic right comes from the bilateral ASA between the home and host states, and it grants the right in principle for designated carriers to operate. The FAOC is the operational permission for a specific carrier, with specific aircraft, on specific routes. Both must be in place before flights can operate. A carrier with traffic rights but no FAOC cannot fly. A carrier with an FAOC but no traffic rights cannot fly either.

## 2. The typical six-stage process

FAOC processes vary in detail across jurisdictions, but the underlying flow is consistent. A typical six-stage process:

**Stage 1: Pre-application enquiry.** The carrier (or its handling agent in the host country) sends a formal enquiry to the host state's civil aviation authority. The enquiry identifies the carrier, the proposed routes, the aircraft types, the proposed entry-into-service date, and the existing bilateral ASA reference (if applicable). The CAA responds with an information package: the applicable regulations, the application form, the document requirements, and the fee schedule.

**Stage 2: Application submission.** The carrier compiles and submits the formal application. This is the heaviest document workstream of the entire process. The typical package includes the AOC and its Operations Specifications (Ops Specs) from the home state, the Air Operator's Manual and Operating Manuals, the Minimum Equipment List (MEL), aircraft documents for each registered tail number, insurance certificates, the carrier's safety management system documentation, dangerous goods training certificates if applicable, security programme documentation, and a great deal more depending on the jurisdiction. Document sets routinely exceed 500 pages.

**Stage 3: Technical review.** The host state CAA reviews the submission. The review usually has two parallel streams: a **documentation review** (does the paperwork demonstrate compliance with the relevant Annex 6 requirements?) and a **state-of-the-operator verification** (is the home state's safety oversight adequate?). The technical review is where applications most often stall. The host state may issue requests for additional information, requests for document clarification, or in some cases requests for evidence of specific operational procedures that were not adequately covered in the submitted manuals.

**Stage 4: Operations and security clearances.** Parallel to the technical review, the host state's operations and security authorities review the application. Operations clearance covers route-specific items: alternate airports, fuel uplift planning, emergency response procedures, ground handling arrangements at the host airport. Security clearance covers AVSEC programme alignment with the host state's National Civil Aviation Security Programme (NCASP).

**Stage 5: Issuance.** Once all reviews are completed and any conditions are satisfied, the CAA issues the FAOC, along with route-specific Operations Specifications. The FAOC typically specifies the authorised aircraft types, authorised routes or route categories, authorised crew designations, validity period (often one to three years), and conditions of operation.

**Stage 6: Ongoing oversight.** FAOCs are not "once and done." The host state retains the right to inspect foreign-flagged aircraft operating into its territory under the ICAO Safety Assessment of Foreign Aircraft (SAFA) framework, and the FAOC itself requires periodic renewal. Any material change to the carrier's operation (new aircraft type, new route category, change of home AOC) typically triggers an FAOC amendment process.

## 3. The document requirements: what is universally required

While document lists vary, certain items are required by essentially every FAOC process:

**Carrier identification documents.**
- Air Operator Certificate (AOC) from the home state, with full Operations Specifications
- Certificate of incorporation or equivalent corporate registration
- Designation letter from the home state CAA (when a bilateral ASA designates specific carriers)

**Aircraft documents (per aircraft).**
- Certificate of registration
- Certificate of airworthiness
- Noise certificate
- Radio station licence
- Aircraft insurance certificate
- Lease agreement (if applicable) and the home state's acceptance of any wet-lease or dry-lease arrangement

**Operational documents.**
- Operations Manual
- Minimum Equipment List (MEL) and Configuration Deviation List (CDL)
- Fuel policy documentation
- Crew composition and qualification documentation
- Safety Management System (SMS) manual
- Security programme aligned with ICAO Annex 17 and the host state's NCASP
- Dangerous goods acceptance and handling procedures (if dangerous goods are to be carried)

**Insurance.**
- Third-party liability
- Passenger liability (often at Montreal Convention minimum SDR levels, but host states may require more)
- Cargo liability
- War risk coverage where applicable

The items above reflect the general case only; the specific document requirements are determined by each host state's civil aviation authority and should be verified against its current published requirements. The submitted document set must demonstrate compliance with the host state's regulations as well as the relevant ICAO Annex 6 standards. Where the home state has filed differences with ICAO under Article 38 of the Chicago Convention, the host state may require additional evidence that the difference does not materially affect safety on the proposed operations.

## 4. Country-specific variations across ASEAN

ASEAN's eleven member states each operate their FAOC regimes under broadly similar ICAO principles, but the procedural details vary substantially. A generalised overview of major jurisdictions (procedures can change; always verify against the current published regulations):

**Cambodia (State Secretariat of Civil Aviation, SSCA).** Cambodia operates an FAOC regime under its civil aviation regulations. The country has been working through significant aviation oversight modernisation in recent years.

**Vietnam (CAAV — Civil Aviation Authority of Vietnam).** CAAV's foreign carrier permit process is relatively well-documented and procedurally structured. Vietnam's market size attracts substantial foreign carrier interest, and CAAV has developed a mature process for handling applications.

**Thailand (CAAT — Civil Aviation Authority of Thailand).** CAAT, restructured after Thailand's 2015 ICAO Significant Safety Concern flag was lifted, operates a relatively rigorous FAOC process under its updated regulatory framework.

**Indonesia (DGCA — Directorate General of Civil Aviation).** Indonesia's FAOC process is administered under PM Perhubungan (Ministerial Regulation) and PKPS (Civil Aviation Safety Regulations). The scale of Indonesia's aviation market and the geographic complexity of operating into multiple Indonesian airports add detail to the process.

**Malaysia (CAAM — Civil Aviation Authority of Malaysia).** CAAM publishes its FAOC requirements (including a detailed compliance matrix) in its Civil Aviation Guidance Material series, which is one of the more accessible English-language resources for understanding ASEAN FAOC expectations.

**Philippines (CAAP — Civil Aviation Authority of the Philippines).** CAAP operates the foreign carrier permit process alongside the Civil Aeronautics Board's traffic rights and economic authority decisions, which means foreign carriers entering the Philippines navigate two separate regulatory processes in parallel.

**Singapore (CAAS — Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore).** CAAS operates one of the more efficient FAOC processes in the region, reflecting Singapore's broader aviation sector reputation.

**Myanmar (DCA — Department of Civil Aviation, under MDCA, the Myanmar Department of Civil Aviation).** Myanmar's FAOC process has been affected by the political situation since 2021; foreign operators planning Myanmar operations should expect substantial procedural complexity and extended timelines.

**Laos (DCA Lao PDR).** Laos operates FAOC processing under its Lao Civil Aviation Regulations, with the Department of Civil Aviation under the Ministry of Public Works and Transport as the issuing authority.

**Brunei (DCA Brunei).** Brunei's limited international traffic volume means the FAOC process is less frequently exercised, but the regulatory framework is in place under Brunei's civil aviation regulations.

**Timor-Leste (AACTL — Autoridade de Aviação Civil de Timor-Leste).** Timor-Leste operates its civil aviation regime under Decree-Law No. 10/2023 (with earlier instruments including the 2006 Civil Aviation Decree-Law). AACTL handles foreign air operator permits as part of its broader economic regulation function. Foreign carriers planning Dili (DIL) operations should expect a process informed by AACTL's relatively small staff and consequently longer informal processing windows.

The pattern across ASEAN is that the **underlying ICAO framework is consistent, but the document templates, the formal processing windows, the inspection scheduling, and the administrative practices vary significantly**. A document package that satisfied one ASEAN CAA last year may need substantial reformatting and additional content to satisfy a different ASEAN CAA this year.

## 5. Timeline expectations

A common practitioner question: how long does an FAOC application actually take?

The key distinction is between the **official processing window** (the time the host CAA takes to process a complete application) and the **total project time** (the time from project kick-off to certificate in hand, which includes document preparation and any back-and-forth on clarifications).

**Official processing window: typically 60 to 90 days.** Most civil aviation authorities publish a processing commitment in this range, measured from the receipt of a *complete and accurate* application. For example, Malaysia's CAAM requires applications to be submitted 90 days before the intended start of operation and issues the Certificate of Validation within 90 days of receiving a complete application; Australia's CASA targets approximately 60 working days; the FAA and Transport Canada require submission at least 90 days before the intended operation date. Singapore's CAAS processes scheduled-service applications notably faster for established carriers. The 60-to-90-day window is therefore the realistic planning baseline for the regulatory processing itself.

**Total project time can be longer**, but this is driven by factors outside the CAA's processing window: the time the carrier takes to compile the document package, the rounds of clarification when the initial submission is incomplete, and the scheduling of any required inspections. The official clock typically starts only when the CAA confirms the application is complete, so an incomplete first submission effectively extends the project even though the CAA's stated processing window remains 60 to 90 days.

The practical implication: submit early (most CAAs require submission at least 90 days before the intended operation date) and submit *complete*. A complete, high-quality application that starts the official clock immediately is the single biggest lever on total project time.

The timeline should be planned alongside the bilateral ASA work and the commercial route launch project plan. The 90-Day Route Launch framework discussed in an earlier post assumed the FAOC was already in hand or near completion; routes where the FAOC is the bottleneck should not have launch dates announced until the FAOC is in the issuance stage.

## 6. Common failure modes

FAOC applications stall or fail for a recurring set of reasons. From experience and from publicly available case reporting:

**Failure 1: Inadequate home state safety oversight.** When the host CAA's review of the home state's oversight system identifies concerns, the application either stalls indefinitely or is rejected. This is most acute for carriers based in states with active ICAO USOAP Significant Safety Concerns or with category downgrades from major external assessments. There is little the carrier can do unilaterally; the resolution requires home state action.

**Failure 2: Document quality issues.** Operating manuals translated from a non-English original, manuals not aligned with current Annex 6 amendments, MEL items inconsistent with the manufacturer's master MEL, expired insurance certificates, or aircraft documents that reference a previous owner are all common reasons for technical review rejection. These are fixable, but each round of clarification adds weeks to the timeline.

**Failure 3: Misaligned designation and ownership documentation.** The bilateral ASA designates carriers meeting "substantial ownership and effective control" criteria. If the carrier's ownership structure has changed since the home state CAA designation letter was issued, the host state may flag inconsistency. This is especially common where carriers have undergone recent foreign investment, joint venture restructuring, or holding company changes.

**Failure 4: Lease structure complications.** Wet-leased aircraft, aircraft under Article 83bis transfer arrangements, or aircraft on cross-border financial leases can complicate the FAOC review. The host CAA must understand exactly which state is responsible for which oversight functions for each aircraft. Inadequate documentation of the responsibility split is a frequent stall point.

**Failure 5: Communication breakdown.** Many FAOC applications proceed through correspondence between the carrier's regulatory affairs team and the host CAA. Email threads run to dozens of rounds. When key personnel change on either side (a CAA officer transfers, a carrier's regulatory manager leaves), institutional context can be lost and the application effectively restarts.

## 7. Practical workflow recommendations

For carriers approaching an FAOC application, three working approaches that reduce friction:

**Approach 1: Build the document package once, customise per jurisdiction.** Maintain a master document set that covers the universal requirements (AOC, Ops Specs, OM-A, MEL, SMS manual, insurance, security programme). For each new FAOC application, customise only the country-specific items (route documentation, host-state-specific compliance matrices, local contact details). This reduces compilation time from months to weeks for repeat applications.

**Approach 2: Engage a local representative early.** Most ASEAN jurisdictions process FAOC applications more efficiently when the carrier has a designated local representative who can attend meetings, hand-deliver documents, and follow up on processing status. The representative is typically a General Sales Agent (GSA) or a local aviation services consultancy. The cost is modest relative to the time savings.

**Approach 3: Treat the FAOC as an asset.** Once issued, an FAOC has real ongoing value. The renewal process is far simpler than initial issuance. Maintain a current document library, track FAOC expiry dates centrally, and ensure that any material changes to the operation (new aircraft type, change in home state regulations, restructured ownership) are notified to the host CAA proactively. Carriers that let FAOCs lapse and then re-apply pay the cost of full initial processing again.

## 8. Three rules for the regulatory affairs lead

For anyone responsible for an FAOC application portfolio:

**Rule 1: Read the regulations, not the rumours.** Industry chatter about "how Country X processes FAOCs" is often outdated. Every ASEAN CAA has published its current regulations and procedures, usually in English or in parallel with English. Pull the actual published document set before assuming anything about the process.

**Rule 2: Document quality is faster than process speed.** A high-quality initial submission processed through a slow CAA finishes faster than a low-quality submission processed through a fast CAA. Investment in document preparation pays back across the entire timeline.

**Rule 3: Plan the FAOC alongside the bilateral, not after it.** Many route launches plan the bilateral ASA work first, then start the FAOC after traffic rights are confirmed. This serialises two workstreams that should run in parallel from the moment a route is approved. The FAOC document set can be compiled in advance of bilateral conclusion; only the final submission requires the bilateral to be in place.

## Closing

FAOCs are quiet work. They do not generate press releases. They do not feature in airline corporate communications. They are the technical, document-heavy, calendar-consuming workstream that determines whether the route the commercial team has been selling for nine months actually flies on the announced date.

For practitioners in regional aviation regulatory affairs, FAOC competence is one of the most underrated career skills. The carrier that processes its FAOC applications efficiently has a real and measurable competitive advantage over the carrier that treats FAOC as an afterthought. The route map you see in airline marketing material is, in real terms, the FAOC portfolio the regulatory affairs team has built and maintained.

The work is unglamorous. It is also indispensable.
