I work in **air planning at a Southeast Asian regional carrier**. My day-to-day covers route development, schedule design, and the regulatory workflow that gets routes launched: bilateral air services agreements, traffic rights, and operating permits.

This blog is where I write about parts of the job that don't fit into slide decks: practical scheduling frameworks, how regional ASEAN aviation regulation actually works, and the gap between "open skies" as a headline and the protocol-by-protocol reality on the ground.

## What I write about

**Network and schedule planning.** Single-aircraft and small-fleet rotation design, day patterns, FDP discipline, block-time realism.

**Traffic rights and ASA negotiation.** Fifth and seventh freedom analysis, regulator coordination, bilateral negotiation timelines.

**Regional aviation policy in Southeast Asia.** MAAS, MAFLPAS, ASEAN-China and ASEAN-EU frameworks, and how these affect commercial decisions.

**Operations from the planner's seat.** What slot coordination, permit applications, and OCC handovers actually look like when a route launches.

I work bilingually in Chinese and English. The underlying analysis behind these articles is often done in zh, but I write here in en.

## Why this blog exists

Most public writing on Southeast Asian regional aviation falls into one of two camps. The first is consultancy white-paper level: high-altitude, low operational detail. The second is news aggregation: current but shallow. Practitioners (schedulers, traffic-rights officers, network planners) don't have a good public-facing place to read about the day-to-day craft.

I write to fill that gap. Partly to think through problems clearly for myself. Partly because the next generation of aviation professionals in this region needs more practitioner-level material to learn from.
