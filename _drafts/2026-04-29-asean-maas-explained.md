There's a persistent myth in regional aviation press that ASEAN has "open skies." It doesn't — at least not in the way most people use the term. What ASEAN has is the **Multilateral Agreement on Air Services (MAAS)**, plus two companion agreements covering passenger and freight liberalisation. Together these form a partial, protocol-based framework that grants specific traffic rights between specific points among states that have specifically ratified each protocol. That qualifier — "specifically" repeated three times — is the entire story.

This article walks through what MAAS actually is, what its protocols cover, where the limits sit, and why **Timor-Leste's accession to ASEAN on 26 October 2025** as the eleventh member state has reopened practical questions for route planners, traffic-rights negotiators, and any operator looking at the Dili (DIL) market.

## 1. The architecture: three agreements, not one

When practitioners say "MAAS," they often mean the broader **ASEAN Single Aviation Market (ASAM) framework**, which sits on three legal instruments:

| Agreement | Year | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **MAAS** — Multilateral Agreement on Air Services | 2009 | Core agreement covering third, fourth, and fifth freedom passenger traffic rights, with six protocols expanding geography progressively |
| **MAFLPAS** — Multilateral Agreement on the Full Liberalisation of Passenger Air Services | 2010 | Extends MAAS to unlimited 3/4/5 freedoms between **any** ASEAN cities (not just capitals or sub-regional points) |
| **MAFLAFS** — Multilateral Agreement on the Full Liberalisation of Air Freight Services | 2009 | Parallel framework for freighter operations |

All three are **multilateral**, but they are not self-executing for any state. Each member must ratify the parent agreement and then ratify each protocol separately. Until ratification of a specific instrument is deposited with the ASEAN Secretariat, that state's operators cannot rely on it — and other states' operators cannot rely on it for routes touching that state.

## 2. A two-minute freedoms refresher

To read the MAAS protocols, you need clean definitions of the freedoms involved:

- **Third freedom**: right of an airline of State A to carry passengers from A to B
- **Fourth freedom**: right of an airline of State A to carry passengers from B back to A
- **Fifth freedom**: right of an airline of State A to carry passengers between two foreign states B and C, as a continuation of, or precursor to, a service originating or terminating in A

Most published bilateral ASAs grant unlimited third and fourth freedoms but treat fifth freedom as a scarce, negotiated commodity. MAAS's significance is that it grants **multilateral fifth freedom** between participating states — but only along carefully defined geographic axes set by each protocol.

## 3. The six MAAS protocols, decoded

The six MAAS protocols expand geography in two dimensions: which **points** are covered, and which **freedoms** apply. Reading the protocol numbers in order, the logic becomes clear:

| Protocol | Geography | Freedoms granted |
|---|---|---|
| **Protocol 1** | Within an ASEAN sub-region (BIMP-EAGA, CLMV, IMS-GT, IMT-GT) | Unlimited 3rd and 4th freedom |
| **Protocol 2** | Within an ASEAN sub-region | Unlimited 5th freedom |
| **Protocol 3** | Between ASEAN sub-regions | Unlimited 3rd and 4th freedom |
| **Protocol 4** | Between ASEAN sub-regions | Unlimited 5th freedom |
| **Protocol 5** | Between ASEAN capital cities | Unlimited 3rd and 4th freedom |
| **Protocol 6** | Between ASEAN capital cities | Unlimited 5th freedom |

The "sub-regions" referenced in Protocols 1–4 are the four established ASEAN economic sub-groupings: **BIMP-EAGA** (Brunei, Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines East-ASEAN Growth Area); **CLMV** (Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, Vietnam); **IMS-GT** (Indonesia–Malaysia–Singapore Growth Triangle); and **IMT-GT** (Indonesia–Malaysia–Thailand Growth Triangle).

MAFLPAS adds two further protocols that flatten the geography entirely:

| MAFLPAS Protocol | Geography | Freedoms granted |
|---|---|---|
| **Protocol 1** | Between **any** ASEAN cities | Unlimited 3rd and 4th freedom |
| **Protocol 2** | Between **any** ASEAN cities | Unlimited 5th freedom |

Once a state ratifies MAFLPAS Protocol 2, the geographic distinctions of MAAS Protocols 1–6 become largely academic for that pair of states — the broader MAFLPAS rights subsume them. This is why MAFLPAS is the practically operative instrument for most intra-ASEAN scheduling questions today.

## 4. Ratification: where the framework meets reality

The agreements entered into force long ago — MAAS itself entered into force on 23 November 2009, and MAFLPAS on 1 July 2011. Per the ASEAN Secretariat's 27 February 2025 status document, all ten original ASEAN member states have ratified both parent agreements and all relevant protocols, though the timing varied substantially. Indonesia ratified MAFLPAS Protocol 2 on 30 July 2013; Lao PDR took until 7 April 2016. Operators routinely build schedules assuming multilateral coverage that, in fact, only became operative for one of the city-pair states well after the agreement's "in force" date.

The lesson for any planner doing pre-launch traffic-rights diligence is to **check the actual deposit date for the specific protocol relevant to the city pair**, not just the parent agreement. ASEAN publishes this; there is no excuse for getting it wrong.

## 5. Why MAAS is not "open skies"

MAAS gets called open skies in press coverage, but the comparison breaks down on several dimensions:

- **No cabotage.** None of the MAAS protocols grant the right to carry passengers between two points within a single foreign ASEAN state. The 8th and 9th freedoms remain firmly bilateral. (Domestic code-share rights and co-terminal rights have been progressively introduced through later protocols to MAFLPAS — Protocols 3 and 4 in the Secretariat's tracking — but these are far narrower than true cabotage.)
- **No ownership liberalisation.** MAAS does not address airline ownership and control rules. Substantial ownership and effective control by nationals of the designating state remains the standard.
- **Capacity is unlimited only on the freedoms covered.** Frequency, seat capacity, and aircraft type are unrestricted under the protocols, but only on city-pairs that fall within the protocol's geographic scope.
- **No automatic designation.** Each operator still requires a designation from its home state and acceptance by the host state. The MAAS framework does not eliminate the foreign carrier permit / FAOC requirement.

In short: MAAS liberalises **traffic rights** across a defined geography, while leaving ownership, cabotage, designation, and operating permissions to bilateral or domestic regimes. This is closer to the Single European Sky's traffic-rights pillar than to a true integrated common aviation area.

## 6. The Timor-Leste question

This is where the framework becomes operationally interesting in 2026.

Timor-Leste was admitted to ASEAN as the eleventh member state on **26 October 2025**, at the 47th ASEAN Summit in Kuala Lumpur, ending an accession process that began with its 2011 application. ASEAN membership, however, does **not** automatically extend MAAS coverage to the new member. The agreements require separate accession instruments, and as of the latest published Secretariat tracking, Timor-Leste has not yet deposited ratification of MAAS, MAFLPAS, or MAFLAFS.

The Timorese Council of Ministers signalled the intent clearly in its 25 September 2025 session, where it noted that accession to the Memorandum of Understanding on ASEAN's Air Services Engagement with Dialogue Partners is **conditional upon prior ratification of MAAS, MAFLPAS, and MAFLAFS**. The roadmap is on the table; the deposits have not yet been made.

What this means for an operator looking at routes to or from Dili today:

- **Multilateral fifth freedom under MAAS or MAFLPAS is not yet available** for any city pair touching DIL.
- **Third and fourth freedom rights remain bilateral.** The existing services from Bali, Singapore, Kuala Lumpur, and Xiamen all operate under bilateral ASAs negotiated independently of the MAAS framework.
- **Any new fifth-freedom routing through DIL** — for example, a hypothetical Kuala Lumpur–Dili–Darwin operation by a Malaysian carrier — currently requires **bilateral negotiation** with Timor-Leste's civil aviation authority, not a multilateral notification under MAAS.
- **The legal instrument count Timor-Leste must work through is large.** By mid-2025, Timor-Leste had ratified 84 ASEAN agreements with 91 instruments translated into Tetum and Portuguese. The aviation agreements sit within this larger queue and are subject to standard ratification timelines.

For network planners with a Timor-Leste interest, the practical guidance is to plan as if MAAS does not apply for at least the next 12–24 months, and to treat any earlier ratification as upside rather than baseline.

## 7. Common misreadings to avoid

Three traps come up repeatedly in commercial discussions about MAAS:

**Misreading 1: "It's a single market, so I can fly anywhere."**
No. The protocol coverage is geographically defined. A Singapore-flagged carrier operating Singapore–Phnom Penh–Vientiane uses MAAS Protocol 4 (fifth freedom between ASEAN sub-regions). The same carrier flying Singapore–Bali–Manado would invoke a different protocol entirely. The protocols are not interchangeable, even when all relevant states have ratified all of them.

**Misreading 2: "Multilateral means I don't need a permit."**
No. Operating permits, foreign air operator certificates, scheduled flight approvals, and slot allocations are all separate processes administered by each host state. MAAS provides the **traffic right**; the operating authorisation is still a discrete administrative act.

**Misreading 3: "ASEAN membership = MAAS coverage."**
No. As Timor-Leste's case makes vivid, the two are decoupled. Brunei joined ASEAN in 1984 but did not ratify MAFLPAS Protocol 2 until 12 November 2010 — a 26-year gap during which Brunei was an ASEAN member with no MAAS-based fifth freedom coverage.

## 8. What this means for practitioners

Three working rules for anyone doing traffic-rights diligence on an intra-ASEAN route today:

**Rule 1: Always check the protocol-level ratification date for both states**, not just the parent agreement. The ASEAN Secretariat's published tracking is the authoritative source.

**Rule 2: Treat Timor-Leste as a bilateral-only jurisdiction** for traffic-rights purposes until formal MAAS, MAFLPAS, and MAFLAFS ratification deposits are confirmed. Plan negotiation timelines accordingly — bilateral ASAs typically run 12–24 months from initial enquiry to signed instrument, with significantly longer timelines if either state requires national parliamentary ratification.

**Rule 3: Distinguish traffic rights from operating permissions in your project planning.** A favourable MAAS protocol position does not eliminate the need for FAOC, slot, or schedule approvals. Build these as parallel workstreams from day one.

## 9. The road ahead

Timor-Leste's accession reopens an interesting question for ASEAN's aviation policy direction. The MAAS framework was designed when ASEAN had ten members; the protocol architecture assumes the four established sub-regions. Timor-Leste does not naturally fit into BIMP-EAGA, CLMV, IMS-GT, or IMT-GT geographically, which raises a drafting question for any future protocol expansion: do the existing sub-regional definitions get revised, or does Timor-Leste join one of the existing groupings (most plausibly BIMP-EAGA, given its proximity to Indonesia and the eastern Indonesian growth area)?

Beyond Timor-Leste, the broader trajectory of MAAS development has slowed. The most recent substantive additions — MAFLPAS Protocol 3 (domestic code-share rights) and Protocol 4 (co-terminal rights) — are gradual liberalisations, not transformational ones. Full cabotage and ownership liberalisation remain outside the framework, and there is no public indication that they will be added in the current ASEAN Community vision cycle.

For now, MAAS remains what it has always been: a partial, protocol-based traffic-rights framework that liberalises specific freedoms along specific axes among specifically ratifying states. Practitioners who internalise that — and read the ratification ledger before drawing schedule maps — will avoid the costliest mistake in regional aviation, which is launching a route on a traffic right that does not yet legally exist.
